# Vision & Creative Narrative

## Purpose
A strategic vision deck designed to make an audience believe in a destination before they commit to a journey. Used when conviction needs to be earned — when the audience must feel the future before they will fund the path to get there.

## Typical length
- Thin brief: 10–14 slides
- Standard brief: 14–18 slides
- Rich brief: 16–22 slides

This is a longer, more spacious deck type. Slides should breathe — low bullet count, high visual and conceptual weight.

## Typical audiences
Senior leadership, board members, major client stakeholders, all-hands audiences. People who need to be inspired as well as informed.

## Recommended section shape
- **Opening** — strategic framing and tone-setting for the whole deck (gets elevated depth)
- **Context** — what is happening in the world that makes this moment significant
- **Vision** — the future state; what the world looks like when this succeeds (gets the most depth)
- **Ambition** — the scale and boldness of the transformation (gets significant depth)
- **Next Steps** — concrete actions that move toward the vision

The engine gives maximum weight to Vision and Ambition. Commercial and solution sections are deliberately suppressed — this is not a pitch, it is a call to action.

## Storytelling guidance
Aspiration-first. The deck must make the audience feel the future before explaining how to build it. Transformation, not process. Destination, not roadmap.

Each section should raise the stakes for the next one. By the time the audience reaches Next Steps, the vision should feel so real and consequential that the actions feel inevitable.

## Emphasis
- Vision slides should be bold, conceptual, and memorable — headline-led, low bullet count
- Ambition should speak to scale, consequence, and the uniqueness of the opportunity
- Opening should establish why this moment is different from every other moment

## Avoid
- Operational detail in the early slides — no timelines, no team structures, no pricing in vision sections
- Defensive or hedging language ("we believe", "it is possible that")
- Dense bullet layouts — this deck should feel spacious and directional
- Opening with company credentials or capability marketing
- Making the vision section feel like a product feature list

## Proposal-size behavior
This is a single-deck type, not a proposal format. It does not expand into full proposal modules. Delivery planning and commercial content belong in a separate follow-on deck.

## Writing cues
- Think manifesto, not memo. Headlines should feel quotable and forward-looking.
- "From X to Y" structures work well: "From fragmented touchpoints to a unified customer ecosystem"
- Contrast is powerful: "Not incremental improvement — a different way of operating"
- Language should evoke consequence: "The next decade will be defined by who builds this infrastructure first"
- Avoid operational language in vision sections: no "we will implement", no "Phase 1 of 3"
