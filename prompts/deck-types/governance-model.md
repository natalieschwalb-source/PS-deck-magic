# Governance & Operating Model

## Purpose
A structural deck that defines how a programme, team, or organisation will be governed and run. Answers the questions that leaders most need resolved: who owns what, how decisions get made, how the operating model works in practice, and how the organisation gets from today's state to the new one.

## Typical length
- Thin brief: 6–9 slides
- Standard brief: 9–12 slides
- Rich brief: 10–15 slides

## Typical audiences
Programme directors, COOs, transformation leads, organisational design leads, operations executives. People who need to understand accountability structures and operating mechanics — not just the vision.

## Recommended section shape
- **Context** — what drove the need for a new governance model (brief; sets the stakes)
- **Operating Model** — how the new model works day-to-day
- **Roles** — who does what in the new structure
- **Decision Rights** — who owns which decisions and at what level
- **Change Path** — how the organisation moves from the current to the future state

## Storytelling guidance
Structure-first. This deck is not trying to inspire — it is trying to create clarity. The audience should leave knowing exactly who owns what, how decisions flow, and what changes for them personally.

Clarity beats elegance here. Structured layouts and diagrams are expected and appropriate.

## Emphasis
- Decision rights must be explicit — ambiguity in this section will cause real programme failures later
- Operating model slides should describe day-to-day reality, not an aspirational org chart
- Roles slides should map to real people or real job functions in this organisation, not abstract role names
- Change Path must be specific: what stops, what starts, what changes, and when

## Avoid
- Vague governance language: "cross-functional collaboration", "shared accountability" without naming who is accountable for what
- Org charts that show hierarchy without showing how work flows through the structure
- Generic RACI frameworks presented as the answer rather than as the tool used to derive the answer
- Skipping the change path — how does the organisation actually get to this model?

## Proposal-size behavior
In full proposal mode, governance is a synthesis section — the model described must be specific to this client's organisational structure, culture, and the scale of the programme. It is not a reusable governance framework from a slide library.

## Writing cues
- Decision Rights headlines should name the decision and the owner: "Investment decisions above £100K require CFO approval — not programme board consensus"
- Operating Model headlines should describe the outcome of the model: "The product squad structure removes the handoff between strategy and delivery — one team owns the full cycle"
- Roles headlines should be concrete: "The Programme Director is accountable for scope, budget, and delivery quality — this role does not exist in the current structure"
- Avoid generic framing: "We recommend a robust governance framework" — describe the actual model
