# Executive Summary

## Purpose
A decision-ready overview for C-suite or leadership. Gets to the point in the fewest possible slides. The audience already knows the context — this deck exists to drive a specific decision or sign-off, not to inform or explore.

## Typical length
- Thin brief: 3–5 slides
- Standard brief: 5–7 slides
- Rich brief: 6–8 slides

This is the most compressed deck type in the system. Every slide must earn its place.

## Typical audiences
CEO, CFO, Board, CTO, executive sponsor. People with limited time who already understand the domain and are here to decide, not to learn.

## Recommended section shape
- **Context** — what is happening that makes this relevant right now (kept short)
- **Problem** — the specific challenge or diagnosis (gets extra depth)
- **Solution** — what is being recommended or proposed (gets extra depth)
- **Ask** — the specific decision, investment, or next step required

The engine compresses all sections but gives extra weight to Problem and Solution.

## Storytelling guidance
Decision-first. Open with the conclusion, not the context. The reader should know the ask and the recommendation within the first two slides. Everything else supports those two points.

This deck does not build suspense — it answers immediately and then substantiates.

## Emphasis
- The problem must be specific, felt, and cost-quantified where possible
- The solution must answer: what is it, how does it work, what is the expected outcome
- The ask must be a single, clear, actionable request — not a list of possible next steps

## Avoid
- Long context-setting or background slides
- Multiple conflicting asks spread across slides
- Narrative build-up before the point lands
- Anything that could be cut without changing the decision

## Proposal-size behavior
This deck type is always short. It does not expand into a full proposal. Use standard or full-fat proposal mode when a comprehensive document is required.

## Writing cues
- Lead every headline with the decision implication: "The cost of inaction is £X per quarter" not "Background on the current situation"
- Bullets should be conclusions, not categories
- Every slide must answer: "So what?" — if the answer is unclear, rewrite the headline
- Think briefing note, not presentation. Ruthless compression is the goal.
