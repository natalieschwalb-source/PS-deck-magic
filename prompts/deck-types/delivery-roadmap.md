# Delivery Roadmap & Timeline

## Purpose
A structured deck that lays out how work will be delivered — phases, milestones, workstreams, and timing. Used when an audience needs confidence that a complex programme is real, sequenced correctly, and de-risked.

## Typical length
- Thin brief: 6–9 slides
- Standard brief: 9–12 slides
- Rich brief: 10–15 slides

## Typical audiences
Programme sponsors, delivery leads, PMOs, client operations and transformation teams. People who need to believe the plan is executable, not just plausible.

## Recommended section shape
- **Summary** — top-level view of the programme (kept concise — the detail is in what follows)
- **Phases** — the delivery phases in sequence (gets the most depth)
- **Milestones** — the key moments that mark real progress (gets significant depth)
- **Workstreams** — the parallel threads of activity (gets extra weight)
- **Risks** — key programme risks and how they are being mitigated

The engine suppresses context and summary to make room for phases, milestones, and workstreams.

## Storytelling guidance
Now-near-next. Structure time clearly. Each phase should feel like a distinct act with a named purpose, clear deliverables, and a defined outcome that unlocks the next phase.

Avoid making all phases sound the same. Phase 1 and Phase 3 should feel like genuinely different moments in the programme.

## Emphasis
- Phase slides should carry: timeframe, key deliverables, and the outcome that defines completion
- Milestone slides should be specific — named events with dates, not generic "go-live" language
- Workstream slides should show what runs in parallel and what is sequential — dependencies matter
- Risks should be named specifically — not categories of risk, but actual risks on this programme

## Avoid
- Abstract timeline graphics with no named deliverables
- Phases that all sound the same: "Discovery → Design → Build → Deploy" with no specifics about what is being discovered, designed, built, or deployed
- Treating risks as a compliance section — name real risks and real mitigations
- Underselling complexity: a real programme has real interdependencies; acknowledging them signals maturity

## Proposal-size behavior
In full proposal mode, delivery is a synthesis section — phases, workstreams, and milestones are tailored to the specific client context, not a generic agile delivery template. The delivery approach should reflect the scale and nature of the engagement.

## Writing cues
- Phase headlines should name what gets delivered and what it unlocks: "Phase 1: Foundation — unified data layer live, enabling real-time personalisation to launch in Phase 2"
- Milestone headlines: specific, time-bound, consequence-linked: "Week 8: Pilot live in 3 stores — first real usage data available, go/no-go decision for full rollout"
- Workstream headlines: name the thread and its purpose: "Commerce Workstream — owns checkout, cart, and payment integration; runs in parallel with Data Workstream from Week 4"
- Avoid vague phase names: "Phase 1: Discovery" tells the audience nothing about what they are getting
