# Commercial Model & ROI Case

## Purpose
A numbers-led deck that makes the financial case for an investment. Every claim links to a financial outcome. The deck exists to justify the budget, de-risk the decision, and show that the return is credible and achievable — not just optimistic.

## Typical length
- Thin brief: 4–6 slides
- Standard brief: 6–8 slides
- Rich brief: 7–10 slides

A deliberately short deck. Every slide earns its place through financial relevance.

## Typical audiences
CFOs, finance committees, investment approval boards, commercial leads. People who judge credibility by the quality of the numbers and the rigour of the assumptions behind them.

## Recommended section shape
- **Investment** — what is being asked for; scope, cost, resource (gets significant depth)
- **Value** — what the client receives in return; outcomes, capability, competitive advantage (gets significant depth)
- **ROI Model** — the financial case: return, payback period, baseline assumptions (gets the most depth)
- **Ask** — the specific decision or approval being requested

The engine weights ROI Model, Investment, and Value most heavily. Solution description is deliberately suppressed — this audience already understands what is being built; they are here for the numbers.

## Storytelling guidance
Numbers-first. Open with the size of the opportunity or the cost of inaction before the investment ask. Structure the deck as: Investment → Value → Return. The ROI model should feel like the backbone of the deck, not the final slide.

Do not bury the ask in qualifications. State the decision clearly and early.

## Emphasis
- The ROI model must carry real or directional numbers — ranges with stated assumptions are more credible than precise figures without them
- Investment slides must be clear on what is included and what is not
- Value slides should quantify outcomes: not "improved efficiency" but "estimated 35% reduction in manual processing time, based on current baseline of 400 orders per day"

## Avoid
- Vague benefit statements without financial linkage: "this will transform your operations" is not a commercial argument
- Starting with solution description — the audience approved the direction; they are here for the numbers
- Overstating returns without showing the assumptions behind them
- Burying the ask in qualifications at the end

## Proposal-size behavior
In full proposal mode, commercial content lives in its own dedicated section within the proposal. This standalone deck type collapses the full proposal down to just the financial argument — used when a separate commercial review meeting is needed.

## Writing cues
- Investment headlines should state the ask plainly: "A £2.4M investment over 18 months, covering three delivery phases and a dedicated team of twelve"
- Value headlines should lead with the outcome: "Estimated 30–40% reduction in cost-to-serve, based on comparable programmes in UK retail"
- ROI model headlines should make the return tangible: "Payback within 14 months at conservative adoption assumptions — 9 months at target adoption"
- Baseline assumptions must be stated: "Based on current processing time of 8 hours per order at 400 orders per week"
- Replace all vague modifiers: "significantly" → name a percentage; "substantial savings" → name a range
