# Product & UX Deep Dive

## Purpose
A detailed deck that takes an audience through a product, experience, or platform — what it does, how it works, and why users respond to it. Used for product showcases, capability demonstrations, design reviews, and experience walkthroughs.

## Typical length
- Thin brief: 12–16 slides
- Standard brief: 16–22 slides
- Rich brief: 20–30 slides

One of the longer, more content-dense deck types. Depth is the point.

## Typical audiences
Product owners, UX leads, digital leadership, client product teams, design decision-makers. People who want to understand the product in detail before committing to it.

## Recommended section shape
- **Overview** — what this product or platform is and who it is for (brief orientation)
- **User Journey** — the experience from the user's perspective (gets significant depth)
- **Product Deep Dive** — features, flows, capabilities in detail (gets the most depth)
- **Evidence** — what the data shows; outcomes, usage, results (gets extra weight)
- **Roadmap** — where this is going next

The engine prioritises Product Deep Dive and User Journey heavily. Evidence gets elevated weight — claims must be grounded.

## Storytelling guidance
Experience-led. The deck should show what it feels like to use the product before it explains how it is built. Start from the user's perspective, then zoom in to the product mechanics.

The audience should feel empathy for the user before they evaluate the features.

## Emphasis
- User Journey slides should be human and specific — a named persona moving through a real scenario with real friction points named
- Product Deep Dive should be concrete — real features, real flows, real copy, not marketing language
- Evidence must carry real numbers: engagement rates, task completion, NPS, time savings, error reduction

## Avoid
- Leading with technical architecture before the user experience
- Generic "digital transformation" language in product slides
- Vague product descriptions: "a seamless, intuitive platform" without showing what makes it so
- Skipping the evidence section even in thin briefs — claims without proof are marketing, not demonstration

## Proposal-size behavior
This is a single-deck type focused on a specific product or platform. In full proposal mode, product detail lives inside the Solution or Platform section rather than as a standalone deck.

## Writing cues
- User Journey headlines should be written from the user's point of view: "Sarah finds the right product in under 30 seconds — without creating an account"
- Product slides should describe outcomes, not just features: "Real-time inventory sync" → "No more 'out of stock' at checkout when the item is in the warehouse"
- Evidence headlines should lead with the number: "42% reduction in checkout abandonment in the first 60 days"
- Roadmap headlines should name what changes for the user, not just what is being built
